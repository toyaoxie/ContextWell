Project at a glance

Central research question

How can AI be harnessed to support human well-being across AI mediated and non AI contexts in a rapidly changing world?

Research orientation

ContextWell is an exploratory doctoral research project. It aims to identify new explanatory mechanisms underlying well-being and human oversight in complex and rapidly changing environments, and to progressively refine and define a capacity based account of well-being.

Core proposition

The project explores and progressively defines well-being as a composite and sustainable human capacity. This capacity enables individuals to engage with, regulate, and harness tools and environments, including AI, across diverse life contexts such as study, relationships, family roles, institutional demands, and AI mediated settings.

Key integration

Well-being efficacy and human oversight are treated as two perspectives on the same underlying capacity requirement. They differ primarily in framing, human functioning versus governance and systems, while sharing a common functional core.

Overall trajectory

The project follows an iterative pathway. It begins with exploratory analysis to surface new explanatory mechanisms, proceeds through conceptual refinement and definition of capacity, and culminates in the development and feasibility testing of a theory informed intervention.

Why this project

Capacity under pressure across social microcosms

Individuals are not defined by a single role. They operate within multiple social microcosms, understood here as distinct but interacting social settings such as work, family, education, institutions, and technology mediated environments, which shape expectations, responsibilities, and decision making across everyday life.

In a rapidly changing world, individuals are required to make decisions under uncertainty and time pressure, manage competing demands across work, relationships, family roles, and institutional settings, and increasingly interact with AI mediated tools that shape feedback, prioritisation, and pace.

Many existing well-being approaches conceptualise well-being primarily as a psychological state or as an outcome measured after difficulty occurs. These approaches offer limited explanatory power for understanding how individuals function within complex situations as they unfold.

ContextWell responds to a different need. It focuses on developing sustainable human capacity for operating well across changing social microcosms, with a focus on capacity development before depletion occurs.

Conceptual foundation

Well-being as a functional capacity

ContextWell positions well-being as a functional capacity rather than a transient emotional state.

Instead of asking only whether an individual feels well, the project asks what capacities enable an individual to sustain judgment, emotional stability, and action across diverse and demanding contexts.

This capacity is composite in nature. It consists of interacting capabilities that are activated and configured differently depending on the situation. The framework emphasises capacities such as situational awareness and sense making, differentiation between interpretations and constraints, emotional stability under pressure, relational navigation and boundary management, and action design within real world limits.

These capacities interact dynamically. For example, situational awareness supports differentiation between options and constraints, which in turn stabilises emotional responses and enables more deliberate action design.

A central premise of the project is transferability. The same underlying capacity structure should be reusable and adaptable across contexts, even when surface conditions change.

Integration

Human oversight and well-being efficacy

Shared capacity demands. A central clarification in ContextWell is that the underlying demands of well-being efficacy and human oversight are substantively aligned.

Well-being efficacy refers to the internal functional capacity that allows individuals to remain clear, emotionally stable, and action capable within context. Human oversight refers to the situational expression of that same capacity, particularly in environments shaped by systems, institutions, or AI.

The distinction between the two is analytical in nature and unified in substance. In practice, both depend on similar capabilities such as maintaining judgment under pressure, differentiating between system influence and personal responsibility, and sustaining agency in constrained environments.

When this capacity is insufficient, individuals experience reduced agency, increased strain, and responsibility confusion. When it is sufficient, oversight remains operational and well-being is experienced as functional stability rather than comfort.

The role of AI

AI as context shaping force and replicable support

In ContextWell, AI is not treated simply as an external technology. It is conceptualised as a context shaping force and as a replicable tool for capacity training and extension.

AI systems increasingly shape how individuals learn, decide, and act by accelerating information flow, producing recommendations with perceived authority, and altering boundaries of responsibility and accountability. These features can amplify capacity depletion if individuals lack the ability to engage with such tools appropriately.

ContextWell explores how AI can be deliberately harnessed to support human judgment and learning in ways that strengthen and preserve human judgment. AI is positioned as a scalable support for repeated reflective practice and capacity development, not as a decision maker.

From this perspective, well-being efficacy functions as a human meta capacity. It refers to the capacity that governs how individuals engage with tools and environments, including when to rely on them, when to question them, and when to disengage.

Research design

From insight to skill through phased development

A key motivation for the project is that insight alone does not translate into sustained ability.

Individuals may accurately understand their challenges and still struggle to act well in the moment, particularly under time pressure, role conflict, or system driven demands. ContextWell therefore focuses on making capacities practisable, reusable, and accumulative, with an emphasis on long term capacity development.

Phase one

Exploratory inquiry

Exploratory qualitative inquiry to surface explanatory mechanisms across multiple social microcosms. Methods include semi structured interviews and scenario based elicitation to surface capacity demands in concrete situations.

Phase two

Conceptual refinement

Iterative refinement that progressively defines and articulates a composite capacity model integrating well-being efficacy and human oversight.

Phase three

Prototype and feasibility

Translation into a theory informed intervention prototype. Feasibility, coherence, and learning value examined through pilot testing rather than large scale efficacy evaluation.

The ContextWell intervention

ContextWell develops a scenario based reflective intervention supported by a lightweight AI dialogue environment. The intervention helps individuals make situational structure explicit, identify strained capacities, practise judgment and sense making in low risk contexts, and translate reflection into feasible actions. The AI component is intentionally constrained. It supports structured reflection and learning at scale without automating decisions or prescribing behaviour.

Intervention logic

Inputs, mechanisms, outcomes

Inputs

The intervention draws on a contextual well-being framework grounded in a capacity based perspective, scenario based reflective prompts drawn from lived contexts, capacity focused scaffolding for action design, and a low stakes AI supported dialogue environment for structured practice.

Mechanisms

The intervention activates situational awareness in concrete contexts, supports differentiation of capacity demands across roles and situations, embeds reflective practice within real life scenarios, enables translation of insight into action through scaffolded planning, and supports accumulation of transferable capacity rather than depletion.

Outcomes

In the short term, the project expects improved clarity, reduced internalisation of complexity, and stronger decision confidence. In the medium term, it aims to support more transferable capacity use across academic, relational, and institutional contexts and reduce the risk of exhaustion. In the longer term, it seeks to contribute to the development of sustainable composite well-being capacity that supports responsible action and oversight in rapidly changing environments, including those shaped by AI.

Scope

Scope, contribution, and limitations

ContextWell makes an exploratory and developmental contribution without assuming a fixed or finalised model.

The project aims to identify and articulate previously under specified explanatory mechanisms linking well-being, judgment, and oversight, to refine and define a composite capacity construct, to translate conceptual insights into a theory informed intervention prototype, and to examine the feasibility and coherence of that intervention rather than its large scale efficacy.

The emphasis is on theory development, mechanism clarification, and design feasibility, consistent with doctoral level exploratory research.

Anticipated limitations

This exploratory study prioritises conceptual depth and mechanism clarity over breadth or statistical generalisability. Findings are intended to inform theory development and intervention design rather than provide definitive causal claims. The feasibility focus supports future confirmatory and scale up research.

Intended audiences

This project is relevant to scholars and practitioners working in well-being and human development, capability oriented intervention design, human centred AI and oversight, and the study of technology mediated social systems.

Doctoral context

ContextWell forms the core of an exploratory doctoral research programme. The study advances conceptual understanding through iterative theory development, uses empirical material to surface and refine explanatory mechanisms, progressively defines and operationalises a composite capacity construct, and develops and pilots a theory informed intervention to test feasibility and coherence.
